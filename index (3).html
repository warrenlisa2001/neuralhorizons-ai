For too long, AI and cybersecurity have been discussed as separate conversations — one about innovation, the other about protection. That distinction no longer holds. AI is accelerating productivity, but it is also accelerating deception, fraud, impersonation and attack speed.

Author: Lisa Warren, Founder & CEO, Neural Horizons AI

AI is no longer experimental. It is being embedded into workflows, customer engagement, analytics, internal communications and decision-making at speed. But the same technologies increasing productivity are also increasing the credibility and efficiency of cybercrime. The issue is no longer whether AI creates opportunity. The issue is whether organisations are building the trust architecture required to use it safely. [Source] [Source] [Source]

“I have seen what cyber pressure looks like from the SOC floor. What changes your perspective forever is the speed. The real danger with AI-enabled attacks is not only that they are more sophisticated. It is that they compress the time between deception, compromise and damage.”

— Lisa Warren, Founder & CEO, Neural Horizons AI

I say this not as an outside observer, but as someone who has worked inside cybersecurity environments and watched attacks unfold in real time. In the SOC, you learn quickly that seconds matter, context matters, and false confidence is dangerous. What concerns me now is that AI is changing the tempo of cybercrime. It is reducing the effort required to create convincing fraud, speeding up attack execution, and stretching the ability of people and systems to distinguish signal from noise. That should concern every leadership team deploying AI without thinking like a security strategist. [Source] [Source]

The scale of the problem is no longer debatable

The data now points in one direction. According to the EY Cybersecurity Roadmap Study, 96% of senior security leaders say AI-enabled attacks are a significant threat to their organisation. Nearly half estimate that at least a quarter of their incidents in the previous year were AI-enabled, yet less than half are strongly confident in their ability to defend against a major AI-enabled breach. That is a serious maturity gap between threat recognition and defensive readiness. [Source]

The Verizon 2026 DBIR reinforces the point from a different angle. It reports that 15% of attack techniques are being bolstered by generative AI, that mobile devices are becoming more successful phishing targets, and that modern attacks are increasingly exploiting vulnerabilities and identity weaknesses rather than relying only on old credential patterns. [Source]

Microsoft’s telemetry shows the same escalation at scale. In Q1 2026, Microsoft Security detected approximately 8.3 billion email-based phishing threats, while QR phishing more than doubled over the quarter. The message is clear: the attack economy is becoming more adaptive, more automated and more credible at scale. [Source]

Why AI scams are escalating so quickly

The most important change is not simply that AI improves attack capability. It improves attack efficiency. It helps attackers write better phishing messages, localise language, mimic tone, summarise information, create more persuasive impersonation and move from concept to execution with less friction. Microsoft has documented how threat actors are operationalising AI across the cyberattack lifecycle, from reconnaissance and resource development through to social engineering and malware support. [Source]

Anthropic’s research adds another layer. In its analysis of 832 banned malicious accounts, it found attackers increasingly using AI in later, more operationally complex stages of cyber activity, including account discovery and lateral movement. That matters because it suggests AI is not just helping adversaries get in. It is helping them operate more effectively once they are inside. [Source]

What this means in practice

AI-enabled scams escalate quickly because they are no longer attacking systems alone. They are attacking trust — in a familiar voice, a credible message, a plausible invoice, a rushed payment request, or a routine-seeming approval flow. Once trust becomes the attack surface, the response can no longer be purely technical.

“The real problem is not simply that AI can generate better content. It is that it can manufacture credibility at scale. It makes scams sound cleaner, look more plausible, and arrive faster than many organisations are prepared to process.”

— Lisa Warren, Founder & CEO, Neural Horizons AI

CrowdStrike’s threat reporting supports this trend on the fraud side. Its 2025 Global Threat Report highlighted a 442% increase in vishing as adversaries weaponised AI-driven deception more aggressively, alongside rising identity-based and malware-free intrusion patterns. That is a reminder that the new attack surface is as much about human trust and institutional process as it is about infrastructure. [Source]

Why agentic AI raises the stakes again

If generative AI accelerated the production of deceptive content, agentic AI raises the possibility of more adaptive attack behaviour over time. This does not mean every attacker suddenly has a fully autonomous system. But it does mean we are beginning to see models used in ways that support iterative decision-making, multi-step execution and reduced dependence on constant human prompting. Microsoft has observed early signs of threat actor experimentation with agentic AI, and Anthropic points to the scaffolding around models as one of the clearest differentiators in higher-risk malicious operations. [Source] [Source]

That changes the defensive burden. The question is no longer whether AI can assist an attacker. The question is whether attackers can coordinate activity more dynamically, move faster between stages, and exploit the lag between signal detection and human intervention. That makes behavioural visibility, governance and response design much more important than they were even two years ago. [Source]

What organisations in the UAE and GCC need to do now

  • Treat cybersecurity as part of the AI operating model. If AI is moving into workflows, decision support, communications and automation, cyber resilience must be built into the system from the start — not reviewed as an afterthought. [Source]
  • Redesign verification for an era of AI impersonation. Payment requests, account changes, credential prompts and executive escalations now need stronger procedural verification because credibility itself is being attacked.
  • Shift from static controls to behavioural intelligence. Modern attacks increasingly evade legacy assumptions. Behavioural detection, abnormal access patterns and identity misuse now matter more. [Source]
  • Strengthen identity as a frontline defence. As attackers increasingly “log in” rather than “break in,” MFA, access review, conditional access and privileged account discipline become even more important. [Source]
  • Govern internal AI use before it becomes shadow risk. EY found that while many organisations say they have governance frameworks, only 20% have fully optimised and embedded them culturally. [Source]
  • Prepare for fraud as seriously as breach. AI-enabled impersonation, BEC and payment diversion can create severe damage without looking like a classic technical incident. [Source]
